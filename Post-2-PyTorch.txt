Last week, we talked about the math behind Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs) and how they flip the Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) paradigm on its head by placing learnable activation functions on the *edges* rather than the nodes.

Today, in Part 2 of our "Build in Public" series, we drop the theory and open the IDE. 💻

I’ve written a minimal, educational 1D KAN Layer in under 100 lines of pure PyTorch. No hidden libraries, no black boxes—just tensors and math.

The secret sauce to making KANs work in PyTorch efficiently is translating the B-Spline mathematics into vectorized tensor operations. 

Here is the breakdown of the core code:
1️⃣ **The Grid**: We initialize a spatial grid representing the control points for our curves.
2️⃣ **Cox-de Boor Algorithm**: I implemented the recursive algorithm to evaluate B-spline basis functions natively in PyTorch, avoiding `for` loops where possible to keep GPU execution fast.
3️⃣ **The Forward Pass**: Instead of a simple `W*x`, we compute the spline bases for every input, multiply them by the learnable spline coefficients (`self.spline_weight`), and sum them up alongside a base SiLU residual block.

What blew my mind writing this: Because the weights are essentially coefficients for polynomials, the network is literally learning the shape of a curve for every single connection. It makes the network far more mathematically expressive per parameter than an MLP.

Have you tried playing with B-splines in PyTorch before? Implementing the Cox-de Boor recursion natively was a fun puzzle. 🧩

⚠️ **Common Error Alert**: A quick tip for anyone implementing this: Watch out for "Dead Gradients." Because splines are only defined within specific grid bounds (like [-1, 1]), if your inputs fall outside the grid, the spline evaluates to exactly 0 and gradients die. Always normalize your inputs (LayerNorm) or write dynamic grid-extension logic!
