In Part 1 and 2 of this series, we broke down the math behind Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs) and built a 1D KAN layer from scratch in PyTorch using B-splines. 

But does placing learnable activation functions on the edges actually make a difference? 

To test this, I set up a classic Symbolic Regression benchmark. I asked a standard Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) and my custom KAN to fit a highly non-linear, complex function:
`y = sin(3x) + cos(5x) * exp(-x^2)`

Here is the setup:
🔹 **The MLP**: 2 hidden layers, width 32. Total Parameters: 1,153
🔹 **The KAN**: 1 hidden layer, width 4, grid size 10. Total Parameters: 112

The Result?
After training both models for 10,000 epochs (lr=0.001), the KAN, using roughly 10% of the parameters of the MLP, fit the sharp curves and rapid oscillations of the function almost perfectly, dropping to a final train loss of just 0.00013. The MLP struggled significantly with the high-frequency components, plateauing at a loss of ~0.0118 and requiring far more depth and width to even come close.

Why did KAN win? 
Because MLPs try to approximate curves using a piecewise sum of straight lines (ReLUs). KANs approximate curves using *actual curves* (splines). 

If you are working on problems involving partial differential equations, scientific computing, or symbolic math, KANs are showing serious promise as a vastly more parameter-efficient alternative to the MLP.
